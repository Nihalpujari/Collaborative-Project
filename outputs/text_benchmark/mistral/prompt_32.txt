**The Loneliness of a Hotdog at a Five-Star Table**

The grand chandelier drips light like melted butter over the white linen, its crystals catching the glow of candle flames that flicker in silent judgment. The silverware gleams, polished to a mirror’s edge, reflecting the empty chair across from him. The menu—handwritten in looping calligraphy—offers truffle-infused this and aged-that, but all he wants is a squirt of mustard and a napkin to wipe the existential grease from his brow.

He arrived with hope. The maître d’ had eyed him with thinly veiled disdain, but the hostess, perhaps sensing his desperation, led him to a corner table where the light was dim and the pity was bright. *"For one?"* she’d asked, and he’d nodded, his bun wilting slightly under the weight of the question.

Now, he sits. The sommelier has already passed him by twice, pretending not to see the way his relish glistens with unshed tears. A couple at the next table shares a laugh over a shared plate of oysters, their fingers brushing like they’ve forgotten what it’s like to be alone. He pokes at his plate—empty, because no one thought to bring him one. The bread basket taunts him from across the room.

A waiter finally approaches, but it’s only to refill the water glass of the businessman three tables over. The hotdog watches the ice cubes clink against the crystal, each one a tiny, indifferent bell tolling for his dignity.

*"Maybe I should’ve ordered the lobster,"* he thinks. *"At least then I’d have someone to talk to."*

But no. He’s just a hotdog. A sad, slightly sweaty hotdog in a world that prefers its meals *deconstructed*.

And so he waits. For a friend who won’t come. For a bite that won’t be taken. For the moment the restaurant closes and he’s left under the harsh fluorescent lights of the kitchen, wondering if he was ever really meant to be here at all.
